Working Papers

Third-Party Discrimination and the Self-Fulfilling Prophecy of Bias
Nicholas Owsley
Job Market Paper

Decision-makers in organizational settings often choose not the most capable candidate, but the one they expect third parties — clients, teammates, shareholders — to accept. Even absent personal prejudice, anticipating discrimination from others can induce discrimination. Using the 2024 U.S. presidential election as a natural shock to beliefs about third-party discrimination, and a pre-registered controlled experiment directly manipulating those beliefs, I provide causal evidence for this mechanism. Across both studies, decision-makers systematically overestimated third-party discrimination, discriminating even when it did not exist — a pattern consistent with the bias blind spot and pluralistic ignorance. Light-touch informational interventions can correct these distortions, suggesting a tractable lever for reducing discrimination.

Race, Sponsorship, and Discrimination in the Housing Market: Evidence from a Correspondence Audit
Nicholas Owsley, Graelin Mandel, and Erika Kirgios
Working Paper

We conducted a large-scale correspondence audit experiment — sending emails to 24,000 real estate agents across all 50 U.S. states — to test racial discrimination against Black homebuyers and whether sponsorship by a credible intermediary can attenuate it. Black buyers received 3 percentage points (10.4%) fewer responses than equivalent White buyers. Contrary to predictions, sponsorship did not reduce discrimination overall, but this null effect concealed a sharp divergence by sponsor race: White sponsors marginally improved outcomes while Black sponsors significantly worsened them. We find that discrimination is driven by agents' expectations of lower buyer budgets — a stereotype that sponsorship endorsements do not address — explaining why the intervention failed to close the gap.

Shortlist Quotas and the Signaling Value of Diversity Policy
Nicholas Owsley, Linda Chang, Erika Kirgios, Katy Milkman, and Aneesh Rai
Minor Revision · Organization Science

Across four pre-registered, incentive-compatible online experiments (N = 12,000), we examine whether shortlist quotas — requirements to include a minimum number of underrepresented candidates in finalist pools — increase diversity in hiring. We disentangle the implementation effect (a more diverse shortlist) from the signaling effect (informing decision-makers a quota was used). We find no evidence of backlash: quotas increased women hired through both channels, and the signaling effect was positive, mediated by perceived pro-diversity organizational norms. Women hired under the policy were not perceived as lower quality.

Slacking After Success: Reference Points, Round Numbers, and Dynamic Motivation
Nicholas Owsley, George Wu, and Donovan Rowsey
Working Paper

Using a regression discontinuity design applied to a comprehensive dataset of U.S. high school track times (N > 1 million), we compare athletes who narrowly beat a round-number time to those who narrowly missed one. Athletes who barely succeeded were significantly less likely to improve in subsequent races and less likely to continue competing over the remainder of the season. A static model of reference-dependent preferences cannot account for these results; we propose a dynamic model in which surpassing a goal diminishes its motivational weight going forward.

Ongoing Work

Preference Uncertainty and Discrimination
Nicholas Owsley, Erika Kirgios, and Edward Chang
In Progress

We examine whether discrimination can arise from decision-makers' uncertainty about outgroup preferences — the sense that they cannot readily anticipate the tastes and wants of a buyer or worker from a marginalized group. If managers or service providers find it harder to gauge outgroup preferences, the perceived cost of working with them may rise, producing discrimination that bypasses negative stereotypes about quality or character entirely.

False Starts: Temporal Landmarks and the Fragility of Fresh Starts
Nicholas Owsley, Ibitayo Fadayomi, and Oleg Urminsky
In Progress

Fresh starts — new weeks, months, or years — motivate goal pursuit by creating psychological distance from past failures. We examine the flip side: whether failures during a fresh start period are weighted more heavily, producing outsized reductions in future effort. Using archival data from UK ParkRun participants and a controlled online experiment, we find preliminary evidence that goal disruptions at temporal landmarks may be more demotivating than equivalent disruptions at other times.
